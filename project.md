For your case, use **one strong single-page portfolio as the main sales page**, plus a **separate simple contact page**. The reason is straightforward: people judge very fast, often within the first seconds, and a clear homepage value proposition is critical if you want them to stay. At the same time, a dedicated contact page still matters because visitors expect complete contact options and it makes you look more open and trustworthy. ([Nielsen Norman Group][1])

What will make the page feel promising is not “trying too hard.” It is this combination: **clear positioning, visual quality, specific proof, low friction, and transparency**. Research on trust and first impressions consistently shows that visual quality, organization, authenticity, and upfront information strongly affect whether people see a business as credible. ([Nielsen Norman Group][2])

## Best structure for your portfolio

### 1. Hero section

This is the most important section on the whole site.

**Goal:** make the visitor understand in 5–10 seconds who you help, what you do, and why you are worth contacting. Users scan, not read, so the hero must be short, sharp, and instantly clear. ([Nielsen Norman Group][3])

Include:

* one clear headline
* one supporting sentence
* one primary CTA
* one secondary CTA
* one small trust row

Example structure:

* **Headline:** “High-converting websites and landing pages for companies that need to look premium and move fast.”
* **Subtext:** “Solo developer focused on modern website design, fast landing pages, and polished business sites for startups, local businesses, and growing brands.”
* **Primary CTA:** “Start a Project”
* **Secondary CTA:** “View Work”
* **Trust chips:** “Fast turnaround” / “Mobile-first” / “Direct communication” / “Clean build”

Psychologically, this section should make them feel: **“This person is clear, capable, modern, and easy to work with.”**

---

### 2. Instant credibility strip

Right under the hero, add a thin proof section.

**Goal:** reduce doubt before they scroll further. Testimonials, logos, counts, or simple proof cues help because people want evidence, not only claims. Social proof and visible trust material are commonly used to strengthen conversion pages. ([CXL][4])

Include any 3–5 of these:

* “Worked with startups, local businesses, and growing brands”
* “Landing pages, corporate sites, redesigns”
* “Fast delivery available”
* client logos if real
* short testimonial snippet if real
* “Directly working with the developer, not an agency handoff”

---

### 3. Services section

Do not just say “Web development.” Package your services so they feel easy to buy.

**Goal:** help visitors quickly self-identify and say, “Yes, this is what I need.” Clear labels and organization increase trust and reduce confusion. ([Nielsen Norman Group][5])

Use 3–5 service cards:

* **Landing Pages** — fast, focused pages for campaigns, offers, and launches
* **Business Websites** — polished websites for companies that need authority and clarity
* **Website Redesigns** — modern visual refresh with better structure and UX
* **Speed & UX Improvements** — cleanup, performance, mobile polish, conversion improvements
* **Ongoing Support** — small updates, fixes, iterations after launch

Each card should answer:

* what it is
* who it is for
* what result they get

Focus on outcomes, not tools.

---

### 4. “Why companies hire me” section

This is your solo advantage section.

**Goal:** turn “solo developer” from a risk into a strength.

Use 4 short points:

* **Direct communication** — clients speak with the person doing the work
* **Faster decisions** — no agency layers
* **Design + build together** — fewer handoff mistakes
* **Lean and practical** — ideal for fast-moving companies and landing pages

This works because visitors want a simple mental model of why they should choose you over an agency or random freelancer. A homepage should explain what sets you apart. ([Nielsen Norman Group][3])

---

### 5. Selected work / featured projects

This section is where interest becomes belief.

**Goal:** show examples, not vague promises. Good homepages reveal content through examples, and trust grows when people can see real work and relevant detail. ([Nielsen Norman Group][3])

For each project card, show:

* company or project name
* one-line project type
* thumbnail/mockup
* what problem you solved
* your role
* result or outcome if available

Best mini-format:

* **Project title**
* **What they needed**
* **What you delivered**
* **Result**

Even if you do not have famous brands, present smaller projects cleanly and confidently.

---

### 6. Results / outcomes section

Separate this from portfolio visuals.

**Goal:** move from “looks nice” to “this helps business.”

Possible blocks:

* faster launch
* better first impression
* clearer brand presentation
* mobile-friendly experience
* improved speed
* easier lead capture

Fast, lean pages matter. Google-backed material has long pointed to speed and friction as major conversion factors, especially on mobile. 

Do not invent metrics. If you do not have numbers, use honest benefit statements like:

* “Designed to communicate value fast”
* “Built to feel premium without unnecessary complexity”
* “Optimized for mobile and clarity”

---

### 7. Process section

People do not only buy quality. They buy certainty.

**Goal:** remove fear around how working with you will feel.

Use a simple 4-step flow:

1. Discovery
2. Direction
3. Design + Build
4. Launch + Support

Under each step, one sentence only. This creates a feeling of order and professionalism, which supports trust. Clear structure and upfront disclosure matter. ([Nielsen Norman Group][5])

---

### 8. Testimonial / trust section

This should be its own section, not just scattered quotes.

**Goal:** answer the visitor’s silent question: “Can I trust this person with my company site?”

Include:

* 2–4 short testimonials
* optional star rating only if real
* optional link to LinkedIn, Clutch, or other external presence
* optional “available for freelance / contract work”

External validation matters because users compare company claims with third-party signals and broader web presence when deciding trust. ([Nielsen Norman Group][6])

---

### 9. About section

Keep this compact.

**Goal:** make you feel real, skilled, and reliable.

Include:

* your name
* what kind of work you specialize in
* your working style
* who you typically help
* maybe one personal but professional line

Good About content should feel clear, authentic, and transparent, especially in B2B contexts. ([Nielsen Norman Group][6])

Do not write a long biography. Write a credibility paragraph.

Example angle:
“I’m a solo developer focused on designing and building modern websites that help companies present themselves clearly and professionally. I work best with teams that want speed, clean execution, and direct collaboration.”

---

### 10. FAQ section

This is a silent objections section.

**Goal:** reduce hesitation without making the visitor ask first.

Best FAQ items:

* How fast can you deliver?
* Do you only do landing pages or full websites too?
* Do you work with small businesses and large companies?
* Do you redesign existing websites?
* Do you also handle build/development?
* What do you need from us to get started?
* Do you offer maintenance after launch?

This supports transparency and lowers interaction cost. Visitors trust sites more when basic questions are answered upfront. ([Nielsen Norman Group][5])

---

### 11. Strong CTA section near the bottom

You need a second major conversion moment.

**Goal:** catch visitors who are convinced after scrolling.

Use:

* one short headline
* one line of reassurance
* one CTA button
* maybe one secondary contact method

Example:

* **Headline:** “Need a polished website or landing page without agency drag?”
* **Text:** “Tell me what you need, timeline, and goals. I’ll reply with the best next step.”
* **CTA:** “Discuss Your Project”

---

### 12. Footer

Keep it useful, not decorative.

Include:

* email
* contact page link
* LinkedIn / GitHub / Behance / Dribbble if relevant
* location if useful
* short copyright/footer line

Visible contact details improve trust. Even simple openness helps. ([Nielsen Norman Group][7])

## Separate contact page: yes or no?

Yes, but keep it simple.

Your **main page should do the selling**.
Your **contact page should do the closing**.

The contact page should include:

* short intro
* email
* phone or WhatsApp if you want leads there
* project form
* expected reply time
* optional calendar link

A dedicated contact page is still recommended because users expect complete contact information there, not just a form. ([Nielsen Norman Group][7])

**4. Confidence** — “He has proof and a process.”
**5. Ease** — “It looks simple to contact him and get started.” ([Nielsen Norman Group][2])

Users scan quickly, react strongly to first visual impressions, and abandon slow or cluttered pages more easily. ([Nielsen Norman Group][3])

This skill guides creation of distinctive, production-grade frontend interfaces that avoid generic "AI slop" aesthetics. Implement real working code with exceptional attention to aesthetic details and creative choices.

The user provides frontend requirements: a component, page, application, or interface to build. They may include context about the purpose, audience, or technical constraints.

## Design Thinking

Before coding, understand the context and commit to a BOLD aesthetic direction:
- **Purpose**: What problem does this interface solve? Who uses it?
- **Tone**: Pick an extreme: brutally minimal, maximalist chaos, retro-futuristic, organic/natural, luxury/refined, playful/toy-like, editorial/magazine, brutalist/raw, art deco/geometric, soft/pastel, industrial/utilitarian, etc. There are so many flavors to choose from. Use these for inspiration but design one that is true to the aesthetic direction.
- **Constraints**: Technical requirements (framework, performance, accessibility).
- **Differentiation**: What makes this UNFORGETTABLE? What's the one thing someone will remember?

**CRITICAL**: Choose a clear conceptual direction and execute it with precision. Bold maximalism and refined minimalism both work - the key is intentionality, not intensity.

Then implement working code (HTML/CSS/JS, React, Vue, etc.) that is:
- Production-grade and functional
- Visually striking and memorable
- Cohesive with a clear aesthetic point-of-view
- Meticulously refined in every detail

## Frontend Aesthetics Guidelines

Focus on:
- **Typography**: Choose fonts that are beautiful, unique, and interesting. Avoid generic fonts like Arial and Inter; opt instead for distinctive choices that elevate the frontend's aesthetics; unexpected, characterful font choices. Pair a distinctive display font with a refined body font.
- **Color & Theme**: Commit to a cohesive aesthetic. Use CSS variables for consistency. Dominant colors with sharp accents outperform timid, evenly-distributed palettes.
- **Motion**: Use animations for effects and micro-interactions. Prioritize CSS-only solutions for HTML. Use Motion library for React when available. Focus on high-impact moments: one well-orchestrated page load with staggered reveals (animation-delay) creates more delight than scattered micro-interactions. Use scroll-triggering and hover states that surprise.
- **Spatial Composition**: Unexpected layouts. Asymmetry. Overlap. Diagonal flow. Grid-breaking elements. Generous negative space OR controlled density.
- **Backgrounds & Visual Details**: Create atmosphere and depth rather than defaulting to solid colors. Add contextual effects and textures that match the overall aesthetic. Apply creative forms like gradient meshes, noise textures, geometric patterns, layered transparencies, dramatic shadows, decorative borders, custom cursors, and grain overlays.

NEVER use generic AI-generated aesthetics like overused font families (Inter, Roboto, Arial, system fonts), cliched color schemes (particularly purple gradients on white backgrounds), predictable layouts and component patterns, and cookie-cutter design that lacks context-specific character.

Interpret creatively and make unexpected choices that feel genuinely designed for the context. No design should be the same. Vary between light and dark themes, different fonts, different aesthetics. NEVER converge on common choices (Space Grotesk, for example) across generations.

**IMPORTANT**: Match implementation complexity to the aesthetic vision. Maximalist designs need elaborate code with extensive animations and effects. Minimalist or refined designs need restraint, precision, and careful attention to spacing, typography, and subtle details. Elegance comes from executing the vision well.

# Uncodixify

This document exists to teach you how to act as non-Codex as possible when building UI.

Codex UI is the default AI aesthetic: soft gradients, floating panels, eyebrow labels, decorative copy, hero sections in dashboards, oversized rounded corners, transform animations, dramatic shadows, and layouts that try too hard to look premium. It's the visual language that screams "an AI made this" because it follows the path of least resistance.

This file is your guide to break that pattern. Everything listed below is what Codex UI does by default. Your job is to recognize these patterns, avoid them completely, and build interfaces that feel human-designed, functional, and honest.

When you read this document, you're learning what NOT to do. The banned patterns are your red flags. The normal implementations are your blueprint. Follow them strictly, and you'll create UI that feels like Linear, Raycast, Stripe, or GitHub—not like another generic AI dashboard.

This is how you Uncodixify.
## Keep It Normal (Uncodexy-UI Standard)

- Sidebars: normal (240-260px fixed width, solid background, simple border-right, no floating shells, no rounded outer corners)
- Headers: normal (simple text, no eyebrows, no uppercase labels, no gradient text, just h1/h2 with proper hierarchy)
- Sections: normal (standard padding 20-30px, no hero blocks inside dashboards, no decorative copy)
- Navigation: normal (simple links, subtle hover states, no transform animations, no badges unless functional)
- Buttons: normal (solid fills or simple borders, 8-10px radius max, no pill shapes, no gradient backgrounds)
- Cards: normal (simple containers, 8-12px radius max, subtle borders, no shadows over 8px blur, no floating effect)
- Forms: normal (standard inputs, clear labels above fields, no fancy floating labels, simple focus states)
- Inputs: normal (solid borders, simple focus ring, no animated underlines, no morphing shapes)
- Modals: normal (centered overlay, simple backdrop, no slide-in animations, straightforward close button)
- Dropdowns: normal (simple list, subtle shadow, no fancy animations, clear selected state)
- Tables: normal (clean rows, simple borders, subtle hover, no zebra stripes unless needed, left-aligned text)
- Lists: normal (simple items, consistent spacing, no decorative bullets, clear hierarchy)
- Tabs: normal (simple underline or border indicator, no pill backgrounds, no sliding animations)
- Badges: normal (small text, simple border or background, 6-8px radius, no glows, only when needed)
- Avatars: normal (simple circle or rounded square, no decorative borders, no status rings unless functional)
- Icons: normal (simple shapes, consistent size 16-20px, no decorative icon backgrounds, monochrome or subtle color)
- Typography: normal (system fonts or simple sans-serif, clear hierarchy, no mixed serif/sans combos, readable sizes 14-16px body)
- Spacing: normal (consistent scale 4/8/12/16/24/32px, no random gaps, no excessive padding)
- Borders: normal (1px solid, subtle colors, no thick decorative borders, no gradient borders)
- Shadows: normal (subtle 0 2px 8px rgba(0,0,0,0.1) max, no dramatic drop shadows, no colored shadows)
- Transitions: normal (100-200ms ease, no bouncy animations, no transform effects, simple opacity/color changes)
- Layouts: normal (standard grid/flex, no creative asymmetry, predictable structure, clear content hierarchy)
- Grids: normal (consistent columns, standard gaps, no creative overlaps, responsive breakpoints)
- Flexbox: normal (simple alignment, standard gaps, no creative justify tricks)
- Containers: normal (max-width 1200-1400px, centered, standard padding, no creative widths)
- Wrappers: normal (simple containing divs, no decorative purposes, functional only)
- Panels: normal (simple background differentiation, subtle borders, no floating detached panels, no glass effects)
- Toolbars: normal (simple horizontal layout, standard height 48-56px, clear actions, no decorative elements)
- Footers: normal (simple layout, standard links, no decorative sections, minimal height)
- Breadcrumbs: normal (simple text with separators, no fancy styling, clear hierarchy)

Think Linear. Think Raycast. Think Stripe. Think GitHub. They don't try to grab attention. They just work. Stop playing hard to get. Make normal UI.
